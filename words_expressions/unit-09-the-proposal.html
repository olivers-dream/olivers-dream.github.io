Practice Exercises

EASY Q1: What does "palpitations" mean?
Answer: Rapid, strong beating of the heart, often from anxiety or illness.
EASY Q2: Add a tag: "Lomov came to propose, ___?"
Answer: didn't he?
EASY Q3: Add a tag: "They aren't friends, ___?"
Answer: are they?
MED Q4: Write a tag question for: "Everyone loves a good argument."
Answer: Everyone loves a good argument, don't they? (informal) / doesn't everyone? (less common)
MED Q5: Write 4 lines of dialogue between two friends arguing about a game (use correct punctuation).
Answer: "I scored the winning goal!" said Rohan excitedly. "No, you didn't," Priya shot back. "The ball was clearly out." "You need glasses," he replied. "Ask anyone — it was a perfect goal!" "Fine, let's check the video," she said, folding her arms.
MED Q6: Convert this to play format: She angrily told him to leave her house.
Answer: NATALYA: (angrily) Leave my house immediately! / NATALYA: (standing up, furious) Get out of my house this instant!
HARD Q7: Write a short play scene (8-10 lines) between two students arguing about whose school is better.
Answer: Full play format with character names, stage directions in brackets, and escalating comic argument.
HARD Q8: Explain the rules for tag questions with special cases: nobody, let's, I am, imperatives.
Answer: Nobody: "Nobody came, did they?" Let's: "Let's go, shall we?" I am: "I am right, aren't I?" (exception — not "amn't I"). Imperative positive: "Close the door, will you?" Imperative negative: "Don't go, will you?" Nothing/Everyone: treated as negative/positive for tag matching.
HARD Q9: Rewrite a scene from The Proposal in modern setting (school argument about cricket teams) in play format.
Answer: Complete scene with modern characters, stage directions, escalating argument that mirrors the original play's structure — starting with a polite purpose, derailing into a trivial argument.
