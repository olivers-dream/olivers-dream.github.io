Chapter Summary

Horace Danby, a 50-year-old respectable citizen who makes locks, has a secret life — he robs one safe every year to fund his passion for rare books. He carefully plans to rob a house at Shotover Grange while the owners are away. A young, attractive woman arrives, claiming to be the lady of the house. She tricks Horace into opening the safe without gloves, convincing him she's forgotten the combination. Horace obliges, leaving his fingerprints everywhere. The real lady of the house looks completely different. The "young lady" was actually another thief who used Horace's own dishonesty against him. Horace is arrested and nobody believes his story.

  • Horace Danby: respectable locksmith and secret thief
  • The Shotover Grange robbery plan
  • The young woman's clever deception
  • Fingerprints, arrest, and poetic justice
💡 Remember It Like This: Horace is like a hacker who carefully plans to break into a system, but gets phished by a smarter hacker pretending to be tech support. His own dishonesty made him vulnerable to someone else's con.

🗺 Mind Map: Key Concepts

Central concept connects to main topics.

Core Concepts

  • Horace Danby: 50 years old, respectable, makes locks, secretly robs one safe per year.
  • He steals only to buy rare books — he sees himself as a gentleman thief with principles.
  • He plans the Shotover Grange robbery meticulously — studies for two weeks.
  • A young woman arrives claiming to be the lady of the house and tricks him.
  • She makes him open the safe without gloves — leaving his fingerprints as evidence.
  • The "young lady" was actually another thief who stole the entire contents.
  • Horace is arrested; his story about the woman is not believed.
  • Theme: "a thief has no right to judge another thief" — dishonesty makes you vulnerable.

Key Literary Terms

  • Situational irony = Horace, the careful thief, is outwitted by a cleverer thief
  • Twist ending = the reader doesn't expect the woman to be a thief
  • Poetic justice = a criminal punished through their own criminal activity
  • Suspense = will Horace get caught? (yes, but not how we expect)

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Writing that the young woman was the actual owner — she was another thief
  • Forgetting that Horace studied the house for two weeks before attempting the robbery
  • Missing that Horace's downfall was removing his gloves — leaving fingerprints
  • Not recognising the double twist: Horace tricked AND the jewels stolen by someone else

Worked Examples

Example 1: How was Horace Danby tricked by the young woman?

Expected answer: She pretended to be the lady of the house and asked him to open the safe, claiming she forgot the combination. He removed his gloves to open it, leaving fingerprints. She was actually another thief.

Solution: The woman appeared confident and authoritative. She called him by name (she must have researched too), threatened to call the police, then "forgave" him in exchange for opening the safe. Horace, relieved at not being turned in, eagerly complied — removing his gloves for better grip. She took the jewels and vanished. When the real owners returned, Horace's fingerprints were everywhere. His story about the woman was dismissed as a lie.

Example 2: What is the significance of the title "A Question of Trust"?

Expected answer: Trust is questioned at every level — Horace's respectable appearance hides a thief; the young woman's helpful demeanour hides a con artist. Neither can be trusted.

Solution: Horace appears trustworthy (50, respectable, makes locks) but is a thief. The young woman appears to be the owner but is a thief. Society trusts Horace (employers, neighbours), but he betrays that trust annually. The police don't trust Horace's story about the woman. The title asks: who can you really trust? The answer: appearance and trust are unreliable in a dishonest world.

Practice Questions

EASY Q1: Who is Horace Danby and what is his secret?
Answer: Horace is a 50-year-old, respectable locksmith who secretly robs one safe per year to buy rare books.
EASY Q2: Whose house did Horace plan to rob?
Answer: Shotover Grange — the home of a wealthy family who were away on holiday.
EASY Q3: Who was the young woman really?
Answer: She was not the lady of the house — she was another, cleverer thief.
MED Q4: Why did Horace remove his gloves?
Answer: The young woman asked him to open the safe because she had "forgotten" the combination. He needed bare hands for the delicate lock work, so he removed his gloves — leaving fingerprints as evidence.
MED Q5: How did Horace prepare for the robbery?
Answer: He studied the house for two weeks — learned the layout, the location of the safe, the electric wiring, the dog's name, and the family's schedule. He was meticulous and careful.
MED Q6: Why does nobody believe Horace's story about the young woman?
Answer: Because the real lady of the house looked completely different (60 years old). Horace had no proof. His own fingerprints were on the safe. A thief's word carries no credibility.
HARD Q7: "A thief got robbed by a thief." Discuss the irony in the story.
Answer: Horace, an experienced thief who planned carefully, was outsmarted by a woman who planned even more cleverly. She used his dishonesty against him — he couldn't refuse or call for help because he was committing a crime himself. The hunter became the hunted. This is poetic justice.
HARD Q8: Compare Horace Danby with the young woman as criminals.
Answer: Horace is careful, methodical, and has a conscience (steals only for books). The woman is quick, opportunistic, and ruthless (steals the entire contents and frames Horace). Horace uses technical skills; the woman uses social engineering. She is clearly the superior criminal.
HARD Q9: What does the story suggest about the nature of honesty and dishonesty?
Answer: The story shows that once you step outside honesty, you become vulnerable. Horace's dishonesty (theft) made him susceptible to the woman's dishonesty (con). He couldn't seek help from the law. The story suggests that dishonesty creates a chain where one dishonest act enables another, and the original wrongdoer often suffers most.
