Chapter Summary

Mathilde Loisel, a beautiful woman married to a humble clerk, feels she deserves a luxurious life. Invited to a grand ball, she borrows a diamond necklace from her wealthy friend Madame Forestier. She shines at the ball but loses the necklace on the way home. Instead of telling the truth, the Loisels borrow a huge sum to buy a replacement necklace and spend ten years of hard labour repaying the debt. When Mathilde finally tells Madame Forestier the truth, she learns the original necklace was fake — worth only 500 francs. The story is a masterpiece of irony by Guy de Maupassant.

  • Mathilde's vanity and desire for luxury
  • The borrowed necklace and the ball
  • Ten years of poverty to repay the debt
  • The devastating twist: the necklace was fake

🗺 Mind Map: Key Concepts

Central concept connects to main topics.

Core Concepts

  • Mathilde Loisel: beautiful but dissatisfied, dreams of luxury, married to a modest clerk.
  • She borrows a necklace from Madame Forestier for a Ministry ball and loses it.
  • Instead of confessing, the Loisels buy a real diamond necklace (36,000 francs) as replacement.
  • Ten years of poverty, hard work, and sacrifice to repay the debt.
  • Twist ending: the original necklace was fake (paste) — worth only 500 francs.
  • Theme: the destructive power of vanity, pride, and dishonesty.
  • Mathilde's appearance transforms — from beautiful to coarse and worn after ten years of hard labour.
  • The story critiques materialism and the desire to appear wealthier than one is.

Key Literary Terms

  • Situational irony = the necklace they replaced was fake; their sacrifice was unnecessary
  • Twist ending = unexpected revelation that changes the meaning of the entire story
  • Poetic justice = vanity and pride lead to punishment (ten years of poverty)
  • Characterisation through transformation = Mathilde changes from dreamy to hardened

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Writing that Mathilde was actually rich — she was married to a humble clerk
  • Saying Madame Forestier knew the necklace was lost — Mathilde returned a real one as replacement
  • Forgetting the ten years of suffering — students sometimes skip to the twist
  • Missing that Mathilde's pride (not confessing) caused the catastrophe, not the loss itself

Worked Examples

Example 1: What is the central irony of "The Necklace"?

Expected answer: The Loisels spent ten years repaying 36,000 francs for a diamond necklace to replace what they thought was real — but the original was fake paste, worth only 500 francs.

Solution: Mathilde lost a borrowed necklace and was too proud to confess. She and her husband bought a real diamond replacement for 36,000 francs, taking huge loans. They spent a decade in poverty repaying debts. The devastating irony: the original necklace was costume jewellery. Their entire sacrifice was based on a false assumption. If Mathilde had been honest, the truth would have been painless.

Example 2: How does Mathilde's character change over the course of the story?

Expected answer: Before: beautiful, vain, discontented, dreaming of luxury. After ten years: rough hands, worn face, coarse hair — hardened by poverty. She accepts her reality.

Solution: At the start, Mathilde is dissatisfied with her modest life, craving luxury and beauty. At the ball, she is radiant and happy — the life she always wanted. After losing the necklace, she does manual labour: washes dishes, scrubs floors, haggles at markets. After ten years, she is unrecognisable — coarse, loud, and aged. But she has also developed dignity through suffering — she tells Forestier the truth proudly. The loss of beauty mirrors the loss of illusions.

Practice Questions

EASY Q1: Who is Mathilde Loisel married to?
Answer: A modest clerk in the Ministry of Education. They live a simple life, which dissatisfies Mathilde.
EASY Q2: What does Mathilde borrow from Madame Forestier?
Answer: A diamond necklace (which turns out to be fake/paste) for a Ministry ball.
EASY Q3: What is the twist at the end of the story?
Answer: The original necklace was fake paste jewellery worth only 500 francs. The Loisels' ten years of sacrifice were unnecessary.
MED Q4: Why didn't Mathilde tell Madame Forestier about losing the necklace?
Answer: Pride and fear of humiliation. Mathilde was too proud to admit she lost the necklace and too afraid of being seen as irresponsible or dishonest. This pride proved far more costly than honesty would have been.
MED Q5: Describe the Loisels' life during the ten years of repaying the debt.
Answer: They dismissed their maid, moved to a cheaper house. Mathilde did all housework, washed clothes, bargained with shopkeepers. Mr Loisel worked extra jobs in the evening. Both aged terribly.
MED Q6: What does the necklace symbolise in the story?
Answer: The necklace symbolises false appearances and vanity. It looked valuable but was fake — just like Mathilde's desire to appear wealthy when she was not. The pursuit of appearances led to genuine suffering.
HARD Q7: "One honest confession could have prevented ten years of misery." Discuss.
Answer: If Mathilde had confessed to Madame Forestier that she lost the necklace, she would have learned it was fake and only owed 500 francs. Her pride prevented this. The story argues that honesty, even when embarrassing, is always less costly than deception. The ten years of poverty were the price of one moment of pride.
HARD Q8: Analyse "The Necklace" as a critique of materialism and class aspiration.
Answer: Mathilde's suffering stems from her desire to belong to a class she was not born into. She craves luxury, beautiful clothes, fine dinners. The ball represents her fantasy come true — but it comes with a devastating price. Maupassant shows that chasing material status beyond one's means leads to ruin. The fake necklace is the perfect symbol: glittering but worthless.
HARD Q9: Compare Mathilde's vanity in "The Necklace" with Mrs Pumphrey's indulgence in "A Triumph of Surgery."
Answer: Both women cause suffering through excess — Mathilde through vanity (wanting to appear wealthy), Mrs Pumphrey through indulgence (drowning Tricki in food). Both refuse to see reality: Mathilde won't accept her modest life; Mrs Pumphrey won't accept she's overfeeding. The consequences differ — Tricki recovers, but Mathilde's youth is lost forever. Both stories critique excess in different forms.
