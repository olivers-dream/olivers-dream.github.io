Chapter Summary

Hari Singh, a 15-year-old experienced thief, befriends Anil, a young, trusting writer who lives on irregular income from writing for magazines. Anil hires Hari as a cook (though Hari can't cook). One night, Hari steals 600 rupees from under Anil's pillow and heads to the railway station to escape. However, he realises that by staying with Anil, he could learn to read, write, and become a respected person. He returns the money. The next morning, Anil gives him a 50-rupee note and offers to teach him sentences — Hari realises the money is still wet (from rain) and knows Anil understands what happened but chooses to trust him anyway.

  • Hari Singh's background and skills as a thief
  • Anil's trust and easygoing personality
  • The turning point at the railway station
  • Education as redemption and Anil's silent forgiveness
💡 Remember It Like This: Hari's dilemma is like standing at a fork in the road — one path (theft) gives quick money but leads nowhere, the other (education with Anil) is slower but opens every door. Choosing to go back was choosing a future over a moment.

🗺 Mind Map: Key Concepts

Central concept connects to main topics.

Core Concepts

  • Hari Singh is a 15-year-old thief who changes names to avoid detection.
  • Anil is 25, easygoing, trusting, lives hand-to-mouth from writing for magazines.
  • Hari steals 600 rupees but has a change of heart at the railway station.
  • The turning point: Hari realises education (reading/writing) is more valuable than stolen money.
  • Anil knows about the theft (wet notes) but does not confront Hari — he gives him another chance.
  • Theme: trust, redemption, the transformative power of education and human goodness.
  • Hari's internal conflict: the thrill of theft vs the desire for self-improvement.
  • Anil's silent forgiveness is more powerful than any punishment.

Key Literary Terms

  • First-person narrative = Hari tells his own story, revealing inner thoughts
  • Irony = Hari employed as cook but can't cook; steals but returns money
  • Character foil = Hari (dishonest) and Anil (trusting) contrast each other
  • Epiphany = sudden realisation that changes a character's direction

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Writing that Anil never found out — the wet notes show he knew
  • Saying Hari returned the money out of fear of being caught — it was about choosing education
  • Forgetting that Hari couldn't cook — Anil kept him despite terrible food
  • Missing that Anil's trust was a deliberate choice, not naivety

Worked Examples

Example 1: Why did Hari Singh return the stolen money?

Expected answer: He realised that education and trust were more valuable than 600 rupees. Learning to read and write with Anil could change his entire life.

Solution: At the railway station, Hari held the money from the train was leaving. He thought about what he was giving up: Anil was teaching him to read and write, to add numbers, and soon he would be able to write sentences. This education could transform him from a petty thief to a respected person. He also valued the relationship of trust with Anil. He returned, placed the money back under the mattress, and chose his future.

Example 2: How does Anil show that he knows about the theft without confronting Hari?

Expected answer: Anil gives Hari a 50-rupee note the next morning and promises to teach him writing. The money is still wet from the rain, which Anil notices but says nothing.

Solution: When Hari wakes up, Anil gives him 50 rupees — a new note. But Hari notices the 600 rupees are still wet from when he stood in the rain. Anil must have noticed the wet notes when he touched them. Yet he says nothing about the theft and offers to teach Hari writing. This silent forgiveness shows Anil's trust in Hari's capacity to change. His smile is "a little sad" — he knows but chooses compassion over confrontation.

Practice Questions

EASY Q1: How old is Hari Singh and what does he do?
Answer: Hari Singh is 15 years old. He is an experienced thief who changes his name with every new employer.
EASY Q2: How much money did Hari steal?
Answer: 600 rupees — a bundle of notes from under Anil's pillow/mattress.
EASY Q3: What does Anil do for a living?
Answer: Anil is a young writer who makes money by writing articles for magazines. His income is irregular.
MED Q4: What was Hari's internal conflict at the railway station?
Answer: He was torn between escaping with 600 rupees (immediate gain through dishonesty) and returning to Anil (long-term gain through education and trust). He chose education, realising it was more valuable.
MED Q5: Why is Anil's response to the theft significant?
Answer: Anil doesn't confront or punish Hari. His silent understanding and continued trust give Hari a second chance. This act of compassion is more transformative than any punishment would have been.
MED Q6: Describe the relationship between Hari and Anil.
Answer: Anil is trusting and generous — he hires Hari despite knowing he can't cook, teaches him to read and write. Hari respects Anil but is tempted by old habits. Their relationship is based on an unspoken understanding and mutual need.
HARD Q7: "Trust can be a more powerful reformer than punishment." Discuss with reference to the story.
Answer: Anil's trust reformed Hari more than jail ever could. By silently forgiving the theft and continuing to teach him, Anil showed belief in Hari's potential for good. This trust became Hari's motivation to change. Punishment punishes the act; trust transforms the person.
HARD Q8: Compare Hari Singh at the beginning and end of the story.
Answer: Beginning: confident thief, sees people as targets, boasts about his skills. End: conflicted, values education, returns stolen money, accepts Anil's trust. The change is not dramatic but internal — Hari begins to see a different future for himself.
HARD Q9: What role does education play as a theme in "The Thief's Story"?
Answer: Education is the turning point. Hari realises that 600 rupees is temporary, but learning to read, write, and form sentences opens permanent opportunities. Anil's teaching represented a path out of crime. The story argues that access to education can redirect lives from crime to productivity.
