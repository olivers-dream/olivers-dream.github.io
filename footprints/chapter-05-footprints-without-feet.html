Chapter Summary

Based on H.G. Wells's "The Invisible Man." Griffin, a brilliant but lawless scientist, discovers how to make himself invisible. He misuses his discovery: he sets fire to his landlord's house, steals from a London shop, takes clothes from a theatre, and robs a clergyman. He arrives at the village inn of Iping, where Mrs Hall is the landlady. He behaves oddly — bandaged face, stays locked in his room, and is short-tempered. When the stolen money runs out, he robs the clergyman and is eventually discovered. Constable Jaffers tries to arrest him but Griffin removes his clothes, becomes invisible, and escapes.

  • Griffin's experiments with invisibility
  • Crimes in London: arson, theft, robbery
  • Life at the Iping inn and Mrs Hall
  • Discovery and escape from Constable Jaffers
💡 Remember It Like This: Griffin's invisibility is like having an anonymous internet account — the freedom from being identified doesn't bring happiness; it enables bad behaviour. Without accountability, even brilliant people can become destructive.

🗺 Mind Map: Key Concepts

Central concept connects to main topics.

Core Concepts

  • Griffin is a brilliant scientist who discovered invisibility through experiments on the human body.
  • He is eccentric, short-tempered, lawless, and misuses his scientific discovery.
  • Invisible footprints in mud are the first sign — "footprints without feet" (no visible body above).
  • He wraps himself in bandages and wears dark glasses to appear normal at the inn.
  • Mrs Hall is curious but tolerant because Griffin pays in advance.
  • When his money runs out, he robs Clergyman Bunting — starting suspicion in the village.
  • Theme: science without morality is dangerous; brilliant minds can cause harm when unethical.
  • The story explores isolation — invisibility doesn't bring power, just loneliness and crime.

Key Literary Terms

  • Science fiction = genre exploring imaginary scientific concepts and their consequences
  • Dramatic irony = villagers can't see what the reader knows (Griffin is invisible)
  • Foreshadowing = early details (bandages, dark glasses) hint at the truth
  • Theme: Power without responsibility leads to destruction

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Writing that Griffin was invisible by birth — he made himself invisible through experiments
  • Confusing the sequence: London (fire, theft) → theatre → Iping inn
  • Saying Mrs Hall was suspicious from the start — she was tolerant as long as he paid
  • Forgetting that Griffin escapes at the end — he is not caught in this extract

Worked Examples

Example 1: Why did Griffin decide to become invisible? What were the consequences?

Expected answer: Griffin was a brilliant but lawless scientist who wanted power. Invisibility led him to crime: arson, theft, robbery, and violence. He became a fugitive instead of a respected scientist.

Solution: Griffin carried out experiments making the human body invisible. But instead of using this for good, he: set fire to his landlord's house, stole from a department store, took clothes from a theatre, and robbed a clergyman. His discovery, which could have benefited science, turned him into a criminal because he lacked moral character. The consequences: isolation, suspicion, violence, and escape.

Example 2: How did the people of Iping finally discover Griffin's secret?

Expected answer: After the robbery, villagers became suspicious. When Mrs Hall confronted him, he removed his bandages, revealing he had no visible face. Constable Jaffers tried to arrest him, but Griffin removed all clothes and became fully invisible.

Solution: After robbing the clergyman, suspicion fell on the strange guest. Mrs Hall confronted Griffin about his odd behaviour and the missing money. In anger, Griffin threw off his bandages, spectacles, and whiskers, revealing emptiness where his head should be. The terrified villagers called Constable Jaffers, who tried to arrest him. But Griffin stripped off all his clothes, becoming completely invisible, and escaped despite the constable's efforts.

Practice Questions

EASY Q1: How did Griffin become invisible?
Answer: Through scientific experiments — he discovered drugs and a process that could make the human body transparent/invisible.
EASY Q2: Why was Griffin wandering the streets of London without clothes?
Answer: Because his body was invisible but clothes were not. To be fully invisible, he had to be naked, which was a problem in winter.
EASY Q3: Who is Mrs Hall?
Answer: The landlady of the inn at Iping village where Griffin stayed as a guest.
MED Q4: Why did Griffin choose to come to Iping?
Answer: He wanted a quiet, remote place to continue his experiments and avoid detection. The village inn seemed safe because Mrs Hall was accommodating as long as he paid.
MED Q5: Describe Griffin's appearance at the inn and why he looked that way.
Answer: He wore a long-sleeved overcoat, bandages around his face, dark glasses, and a wide-brimmed hat. All this was to hide his invisibility — without these coverings, his body would appear headless or bodiless.
MED Q6: How did Griffin rob the clergyman's house?
Answer: Being invisible, Griffin entered the clergyman's study while he and his wife were asleep. He stole money from the desk. The clergyman heard noises but found no one — the room appeared empty.
HARD Q7: "Griffin was a brilliant scientist but not a good human being." Explain.
Answer: Griffin had the genius to make the human body invisible — a remarkable scientific achievement. But he lacked ethics: he committed arson, theft, assault, and robbery. Instead of benefiting humanity with his discovery, he used it for personal gain and revenge. Brilliance without morality is dangerous.
HARD Q8: What does the story suggest about the relationship between science and ethics?
Answer: The story warns that scientific power without ethical restraint leads to disaster. Griffin's invisibility is a metaphor for unchecked power — it could have been used for good (medicine, defence) but instead served crime. The story argues that scientific advancement must be guided by moral responsibility.
HARD Q9: Compare Griffin's invisibility with Horace Danby's "respectability" as disguises.
Answer: Griffin uses physical invisibility to commit crimes; Horace uses social respectability. Both are disguises that hide criminal intent. Both eventually fail — Griffin is discovered and hunted; Horace is caught through fingerprints. The stories suggest that dishonesty, whether hidden by science or society, is eventually exposed.
