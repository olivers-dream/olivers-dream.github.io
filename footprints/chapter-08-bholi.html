Chapter Summary

Bholi (real name: Sulekha) is the youngest of seven children in a village family. A childhood fall left her brain slightly damaged, causing her to stammer, and pockmarks from smallpox marred her face. Her family considered her a burden. When a new school opens in the village, her father, Numberdar Ramlal, reluctantly sends her because no one else would go. A kind teacher encourages Bholi and transforms her life through education. Years later, when her father arranges her marriage to an older, greedy man (Bishamber Nath) who demands dowry on seeing her pockmarks, Bholi refuses the marriage, asserting her dignity. She decides to become a teacher instead.

  • Bholi's disability and family neglect
  • The transformative power of the teacher
  • Bishamber's dowry demand at the wedding
  • Bholi's rebellion and choice to teach

🗺 Mind Map: Key Concepts

Central concept connects to main topics.

Core Concepts

  • Bholi (Sulekha) — brain-damaged from a childhood fall, stammering, pockmarked from smallpox.
  • She is the neglected, "dumb" child in a family of seven. Nobody expects anything from her.
  • Education transforms her: a kind teacher at the village school encourages her and builds her confidence.
  • The teacher says "Don't be afraid" — these words become Bholi's life mantra.
  • Bishamber Nath: a man who agrees to marry Bholi without dowry but demands 5,000 on seeing her face.
  • Bholi refuses the marriage, saying "I won't marry this greedy, heartless man."
  • She decides to stay unmarried and serve as a village teacher — asserting her independence.
  • Themes: women's empowerment, the transformative power of education, standing against social evils.

Key Literary Terms

  • Characterisation through transformation = Bholi from timid to assertive
  • Symbolism: education = freedom and voice for the oppressed
  • Socially relevant themes = dowry, gender discrimination, disability
  • Third-person narrative = omniscient narrator reveals inner thoughts

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Writing that Bholi's family supported her education — they sent her only because no one else would go
  • Confusing Bholi's name — her real name is Sulekha; Bholi (simpleton) is a nickname
  • Forgetting that Bishamber first agreed without dowry but demanded money on seeing her pockmarks
  • Missing that Bholi chose to be a teacher — it was her own decision, not forced

Worked Examples

Example 1: How did education transform Bholi's life?

Expected answer: Education gave Bholi confidence, voice, and independence. She went from a stammering, scared girl to someone who could refuse an unjust marriage and choose to become a teacher.

Solution: Before school: Bholi was neglected, mocked, and had no self-worth. At school: a kind teacher encouraged her, made her speak, and told her she would be the most learned in the village. The teacher's words "Don't be afraid" became her strength. After education: Bholi could stand up against her greedy groom, reject the marriage publicly, and declare she would teach at the village school. Education didn't just teach her to read — it gave her a voice and dignity.

Example 2: Why did Bholi refuse to marry Bishamber Nath?

Expected answer: Because he was greedy and heartless — he demanded 5,000 rupees dowry after seeing her pockmarked face. Bholi said she would not marry a man who was greedy enough to sell himself.

Solution: Bishamber Nath had agreed to marry Bholi without dowry. But at the wedding, when he saw her pockmarked face, he demanded 5,000 rupees. Bholi's father, desperate, agreed. But Bholi, empowered by education, refused. She threw the garland into the fire and said she would not marry this greedy man. She chose self-respect over a marriage arranged for social convention.

Practice Questions

EASY Q1: What is Bholi's real name?
Answer: Sulekha. "Bholi" (simpleton) was a nickname given because of her slow development and stammering.
EASY Q2: Why was Bholi neglected by her family?
Answer: She was brain-damaged from a childhood fall, stammered, and had pockmarks from smallpox. Her family saw her as a burden.
EASY Q3: Who encouraged Bholi at school?
Answer: A kind, gentle teacher who told her not to be afraid and promised she would become the most learned woman in the village.
MED Q4: Why did Ramlal agree to send Bholi to school?
Answer: Not because he valued girls' education, but because the Tehsildar insisted. Since Bholi was "useless" in his eyes, sending her wouldn't be a loss. Ironically, this reluctant decision transformed her life.
MED Q5: Explain the significance of the teacher's words "Don't be afraid."
Answer: These words gave Bholi courage for the first time. No one had ever encouraged her. The teacher's gentle kindness and faith in Bholi's ability planted the seed of confidence that grew into the strength to refuse Bishamber.
MED Q6: What role does dowry play in the story?
Answer: Bishamber agrees to marry Bholi without dowry (because she's "damaged goods" in society's eyes). But when he sees her face, he demands 5,000. This exposes dowry as a cruel social practice that reduces women to commodities.
HARD Q7: "Bholi" is a story about the power of one good teacher. Discuss.
Answer: One teacher changed Bholi's entire life trajectory. At home, she was neglected and mocked. At school, the teacher saw potential, encouraged speech, and built confidence. Years later, Bholi's courage to refuse the marriage and choose teaching directly traces back to that teacher's kindness. The story argues that one caring educator can transform lives more than any policy.
HARD Q8: How does the story critique social attitudes towards disability and gender?
Answer: Bholi is doubly marginalised — as a girl and as someone with disabilities. Her family sees her as unmarriageable and worthless. Society treats her pockmarks as grounds for dowry demands. The story critiques: (1) families that devalue disabled children, (2) men who see marriage as a transaction, (3) communities that don't educate girls. Bholi's rebellion challenges all these attitudes.
HARD Q9: Compare Bholi's empowerment through education with Hari Singh's transformation in "The Thief's Story."
Answer: Both are transformed by education. Hari chose to stay with Anil because learning to read and write offered him a better future than crime. Bholi's education gave her the voice to reject an unjust marriage. Both stories argue that education is the most powerful agent of social change — it saves Hari from a life of crime and Bholi from a life of subjugation.
