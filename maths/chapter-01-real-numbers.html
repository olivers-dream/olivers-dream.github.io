Book Chapter Summary (Easy Language)

This chapter builds the base of number theory: Euclid algorithm, Fundamental Theorem of Arithmetic, and decimal expansion of rationals. You learn how to find HCF/LCM fast, and how prime factor patterns decide terminating or recurring decimals.

  • Start with Euclid Division Lemma and understand how repeated division gives HCF quickly.
  • Study prime factorization and why each composite number has a unique prime breakdown.
  • Connect prime factors to HCF/LCM relations used in direct board sums.
  • Classify decimals as terminating or recurring using denominator factors in lowest form.
💡 Remember It Like This: Imagine cutting two ribbon lengths into equal biggest pieces. The biggest piece size that perfectly fits both is HCF.

Key Points You Must Know

  • Euclid division lemma: a = bq + r, where 0 ≤ r < b.
  • HCF via repeated remainder method is exam-favorite.
  • Every composite number has unique prime factorization.
  • Rational numbers terminate only when denominator has factors 2 and/or 5 (in lowest form).

Formula / Theorem Quick Box

  • LCM × HCF = Product of two numbers
  • For p/q in lowest form: terminating decimal iff q = 2^m 5^n

Note to Remember

  • Always reduce fraction before termination test.
  • Euclid stops only at remainder 0.
  • Prime factors decide decimal behavior.

Worked Examples (Attempt First, Then Reveal)

Example 1: Use Euclid's algorithm to find HCF(1071, 462).

Expected final answer format: 21

Step-by-step solution: 1071 = 462 × 2 + 147 | 462 = 147 × 3 + 21 | 147 = 21 × 7 + 0 | ∴ HCF = 21

Example 2: Will 7^n ever end in 0 for any natural n?

Expected final answer format: No

Step-by-step solution: Ending in 0 means number has factor 10 = 2×5. But 7^n has only factor 7, so impossible.

Practice with Solutions

EASY Q1: Find HCF of 135 and 225.
Solution: 45
EASY Q2: Find the HCF of 48 and 64.
Solution: 16
EASY Q3: Is 1.23 terminating or recurring?
Solution: Terminating
MED Q4: Show that √5 is irrational.
Solution: Assume √5 = p/q in lowest form. Then 5 divides p and q, contradiction.
MED Q5: Find LCM of 12 and 18 using HCF.
Solution: 36
MED Q6: Will 2^n × 5^m × 7 ever give a terminating decimal?
Solution: No, because 7 is a prime factor.
HARD Q7: Find LCM of 306 and 657.
Solution: HCF=9, so LCM=(306×657)/9 = 22338.
HARD Q8: Prove that √2 + √3 is irrational.
Solution: Assume rational, square both sides, derive contradiction.
HARD Q9: Find HCF and LCM of 117 and 221.
Solution: HCF=13, LCM=1989
