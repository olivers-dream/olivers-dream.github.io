Core Concepts

  • India has ~47,000 plant species (10th in world) and ~90,000 animal species.
  • IUCN classification: Normal, Endangered, Vulnerable, Rare, Endemic, Extinct.
  • Reserved Forests (more than half), Protected Forests, Unclassed Forests.
  • Causes of depletion: colonial timber trade, agricultural expansion, mining, habitat loss.
  • Key species: tiger, one-horned rhino, lion-tailed macaque, sangai deer.
  • Community conservation examples: Bishnoi community (Rajasthan), Joint Forest Management.

Key Facts & Figures

  • Forest Cover Target = 33% of total area
  • Biodiversity = Variety of species × Genetic diversity

Worked Examples

Example 1: Why are forests called biodiversity hotspots?

Expected answer: They harbour a huge variety of plant and animal species in small areas

Solution: Western Ghats, Northeast India are biodiversity hotspots with high species concentration under threat.

Example 2: What is Joint Forest Management?

Expected answer: Government and local communities manage forests together

Solution: Started in 1988 policy. Local communities protect forests and share benefits like non-timber products.

Practice Questions

EASY Q1: What percentage should forests cover?
Answer: 33% of total land area
EASY Q2: Name two endangered species from India.
Answer: Bengal tiger, one-horned rhinoceros
EASY Q3: Name two types of forests in India.
Answer: Reserved forests and Protected forests
MED Q4: What are the IUCN categories?
Answer: Normal, Endangered, Vulnerable, Rare, Endemic, Extinct
MED Q5: Explain the Chipko movement.
Answer: Villagers hugged trees to prevent felling by contractors in the Himalayas; a grassroots conservation effort.
MED Q6: Why did colonial policies damage Indian forests?
Answer: Timber extraction for railways, shipbuilding; converting forests to plantations for commercial use.
HARD Q7: How does the Bishnoi community conserve nature?
Answer: Protect trees and wildlife as part of religious belief. Famous for sacrificing lives to save Khejri trees.
HARD Q8: What is the difference between endemic and endangered species?
Answer: Endemic: found only in a particular area. Endangered: species whose population is declining dangerously.
HARD Q9: Discuss the role of stakeholders in forest conservation.
Answer: Local communities, government, industries, and NGOs each have roles: regulation, protection, sustainable use, awareness.
