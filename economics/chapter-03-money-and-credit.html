Chapter Summary

Understand modern money, the banking system, formal and informal credit, and how credit can help or trap people depending on the terms.

  • Barter to modern money evolution
  • Banking: deposits, loans, and reserve ratio
  • Formal vs informal credit and debt traps
  • Self-Help Groups and financial inclusion

Core Concepts

  • Barter system: exchange goods for goods. Problem: double coincidence of wants.
  • Money: accepted medium of exchange. Modern money = currency (paper notes, coins) authorised by government.
  • Demand deposits: money in bank accounts. Cheques transfer money without cash. Banks keep ~15% as reserves.
  • Banks use deposits to give loans (at higher interest) — this is how they earn profit.
  • Formal credit: banks, cooperatives — regulated by RBI. Lower interest (10-12%), proper documentation.
  • Informal credit: moneylenders, traders, relatives — no regulation, high interest (36-60%+), exploitative.
  • Credit can be beneficial (farmer buys seeds → good crop → repays) or create debt trap (crop fails → can't repay → borrows more).
  • Self-Help Groups (SHGs): 15-20 women pool savings, lend to each other. Linked to banks. Grameen Bank model (Bangladesh).
  • RBI supervises formal sector: ensures banks maintain reserves, follow lending norms, prevent fraud.

Key Facts & Figures

  • Reserve ratio: banks keep ~15% as cash reserves
  • Formal credit interest: 10-12%
  • Informal credit interest: 36-60%+
  • SHG: 15-20 members pool savings

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Thinking barter has no disadvantage (double coincidence)
  • Believing all credit is bad — it can be productive if terms are fair
  • Confusing formal (bank) and informal (moneylender) credit

Worked Examples

Example 1: What is a debt trap?

Expected answer: When a borrower can't repay and keeps borrowing at higher interest, falling deeper into debt

Solution: Farmer borrows from moneylender at 60%. Crop fails. Can't repay principal + interest. Borrows more to repay. Interest accumulates. Loses land. Cycle continues.

Example 2: How do SHGs help the poor?

Expected answer: Members pool savings, lend at low interest, access bank loans, build financial independence

Solution: 15-20 women save small amounts regularly. Pool is used for emergency loans to members at low interest. After establishing creditworthiness, the SHG can borrow from banks at reasonable rates.

Practice Questions

EASY Q1: What is the barter system?
Answer: Exchanging goods for goods directly, without using money.
EASY Q2: What are demand deposits?
Answer: Money deposited in bank accounts that can be withdrawn on demand.
EASY Q3: What is a cheque?
Answer: Paper instructing bank to pay a specific amount from your account to another person/account.
MED Q4: How do banks make money?
Answer: Accept deposits (pay low interest) → lend to borrowers (charge higher interest). Difference is bank's profit.
MED Q5: Why is informal credit dangerous?
Answer: No regulation, very high interest rates (36-60%+), can lead to debt trap, exploitation of poor.
MED Q6: What is the role of RBI?
Answer: Supervises banks, ensures adequate reserves, controls money supply, regulates lending rates, prevents fraud.
HARD Q7: Why do poor people still rely on informal credit?
Answer: Banks need collateral and documentation, process is slow. Moneylenders give instant loans, no paperwork. Poor have no assets for collateral.
HARD Q8: How can formal credit be expanded to rural areas?
Answer: More bank branches, SHGs linked to banks, mobile banking, simplified loans, cooperative banks, government schemes like Mudra Yojana.
HARD Q9: Compare the Grameen Bank model with traditional banking.
Answer: Grameen: no collateral needed, targets women, group lending, peer pressure for repayment. Traditional: collateral required, individual loans, may not reach poor.
