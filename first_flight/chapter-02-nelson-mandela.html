Chapter Summary

Prose: An autobiography extract describing Nelson Mandela's inauguration as South Africa's first Black president on 10 May 1994. He reflects on the long struggle against apartheid, the sacrifices of freedom fighters, and how oppression created the thirst for freedom. He distinguishes between the courage to face fear (true bravery) and the absence of fear. Poem: "A Tiger in the Zoo" by Leslie Norris contrasts a tiger's life in a zoo (confined, powerless, pacing in a cage) with its natural habitat (lurking in the jungle, terrorising villagers). The poem evokes sympathy for caged animals.

  • Mandela's inauguration and the end of apartheid
  • Twin obligations and the concept of true courage
  • "A Tiger in the Zoo": cage vs jungle imagery
  • Freedom as indivisible — both oppressor and oppressed
💡 Remember It Like This: Mandela's journey is like a marathon — the inauguration was the finish line, but the real story is the years of running through pain, doubt, and imprisonment. The trophy is freedom for all, not just for one person.

Core Concepts

  • Inauguration day: 10 May 1994 at Union Buildings, Pretoria — rainbow gathering of nations.
  • Mandela says the oppressor and oppressed are both robbed of their humanity.
  • True courage: not the absence of fear, but the triumph over it.
  • Apartheid: system of racial discrimination in South Africa that denied rights to non-whites.
  • Mandela acknowledges the extraordinary courage of comrades like Oliver Tambo, Walter Sisulu, Chief Luthuli.
  • "A Tiger in the Zoo" — juxtaposition: cage vs jungle, powerlessness vs power.
  • The tiger in the zoo ignores visitors, stares at stars — symbolises longing for freedom.
  • Literary devices in poem: contrast, imagery, personification, enjambment.

Key Literary Terms

  • Apartheid = Racial segregation + Discrimination by law (1948-1994)
  • Autobiography = first-person account of one's own life
  • Juxtaposition = placing two contrasting things side by side for effect
  • Enjambment = continuation of a sentence beyond the end of a line

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Writing that Mandela became president without struggle — the extract emphasises decades of sacrifice
  • Confusing biography (someone else writes) with autobiography (self-written)
  • Missing that the poem's tiger symbolises all creatures denied freedom, not just animals
  • Forgetting that Mandela says both oppressor and oppressed lose their humanity

Worked Examples

Example 1: What does Mandela mean by saying "the oppressor and the oppressed alike are robbed of their humanity"?

Expected answer: Both the oppressor (who practices injustice) and the oppressed (who suffers it) lose their humanity — one through cruelty, the other through suffering.

Solution: Mandela believes that apartheid dehumanised everyone. The oppressor becomes heartless and morally corrupt by denying others their rights. The oppressed are reduced to less than human by being denied dignity. True freedom means liberating both sides.

Example 2: How does the poet contrast the tiger in the zoo with the tiger in the wild?

Expected answer: In the wild, the tiger lurks in shadows, terrorises villages, is powerful and free. In the zoo, it paces in a cage, ignores visitors, and longingly stares at stars.

Solution: In the jungle: "lurking in shadow, sliding through long grass" — powerful imagery of stealth and freedom. In the zoo: "stalks in his vivid stripes, few steps of his cage" — confined movement. The contrast highlights the cruelty of caging wild animals and the loss of their natural dignity.

Practice Questions

EASY Q1: When was Nelson Mandela inaugurated as president?
Answer: 10 May 1994, at the Union Buildings, Pretoria, South Africa.
EASY Q2: What is apartheid?
Answer: A system of racial discrimination in South Africa that segregated and denied rights to non-white citizens.
EASY Q3: Where is the tiger in the poem and what does it do?
Answer: The tiger is in a zoo cage. It paces back and forth in the limited space and stares at the stars at night.
MED Q4: What does Mandela mean by "twin obligations" every person has?
Answer: Every person has obligations to family, parents, and community AND obligations to the people, the nation, and humanity. In an unjust society like apartheid, one couldn't fulfil both.
MED Q5: Explain the significance of the rainbow gathering at the inauguration.
Answer: Representatives of many nations came together, symbolising unity in diversity after years of racial segregation. The "rainbow nation" idea represents all races living together in harmony.
MED Q6: What is the central theme of "A Tiger in the Zoo"?
Answer: The poem highlights the loss of freedom and dignity when wild animals are caged. It contrasts the tiger's powerful natural life with its helpless existence in captivity, evoking sympathy and questioning human cruelty.
HARD Q7: How does Mandela distinguish between courage and the absence of fear?
Answer: Mandela says courage is not the absence of fear but the triumph over it. A brave person is not one who doesn't feel afraid, but one who conquers that fear. He learned this from comrades who faced danger despite being afraid.
HARD Q8: "Freedom is indivisible." Discuss with reference to Mandela's views.
Answer: Mandela realised that the chains on any one person are chains on all. The oppressor is not free because he is trapped by hatred and prejudice. True freedom means freedom for everyone — both Black and White. Apartheid enslaved all South Africans morally and spiritually.
HARD Q9: What deeper message about captivity does "A Tiger in the Zoo" convey beyond animal rights?
Answer: The poem is a metaphor for any form of imprisonment — political, social, or emotional. The tiger represents anyone whose freedom is curtailed by powerful forces. Its dignified silence (ignoring visitors, gazing at stars) symbolises the unbreakable spirit of the oppressed.
