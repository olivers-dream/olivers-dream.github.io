Chapter Summary

Prose: Lencho, a poor farmer, writes a letter to God asking for 100 pesos after a devastating hailstorm destroys his crop. The postmaster and employees, moved by his faith, collect money and send it to Lencho. However, Lencho receives only 70 pesos and writes another letter to God complaining that the post office employees must have stolen the remaining 30 pesos. The story highlights unshakeable faith and irony. Poems: "Dust of Snow" by Robert Frost shows how a small natural event (a crow shaking snow from a hemlock tree) changes the poet's mood and saves a wasted day. "Fire and Ice" by Robert Frost discusses how the world might end — through fire (desire/passion) or ice (hatred/coldness).

  • Lencho's unshakeable faith and the hailstorm
  • Role of the postmaster and dramatic irony
  • "Dust of Snow": symbolism of crow and hemlock
  • "Fire and Ice": desire vs hatred as forces of destruction
💡 Remember It Like This: Lencho's situation is like calling customer service for help, getting excellent support, but then blaming the same support team when the solution isn't 100% perfect — you miss the kindness right in front of you.

🗺 Mind Map: Key Concepts

Central concept connects to main topics.

Core Concepts

  • Lencho's faith in God is absolute — he addresses the letter simply "To God".
  • Dramatic irony: Lencho blames the very people who helped him (post office employees).
  • The postmaster is moved by Lencho's faith and decides to help him maintain it.
  • Theme: The power of faith, irony of gratitude, and human kindness.
  • "Dust of Snow" — literary devices: alliteration, inversion, symbolism (crow = bad omen, hemlock = poison, yet bring positive change).
  • "Fire and Ice" — fire symbolises desire/greed/passion; ice symbolises hatred/coldness/indifference.
  • Robert Frost uses simple language to convey deep philosophical ideas.
  • Both poems are examples of short lyric poetry with compressed meaning.

Key Literary Terms

  • Irony = when the outcome is opposite of what is expected
  • Symbolism: using objects to represent abstract ideas
  • Alliteration: repetition of consonant sounds at the start of words (e.g., "dust of snow")
  • Hyperbole: exaggeration for emphasis (Lencho's absolute faith)

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Writing that Lencho received money from God directly — it was the post office employees
  • Confusing dramatic irony with situational irony in this chapter
  • Ignoring the symbolism of crow and hemlock in "Dust of Snow" — they are traditionally negative symbols used positively
  • Forgetting that "Fire and Ice" is about the destruction of the world, not literal fire/ice

Worked Examples

Example 1: Why did Lencho write a letter to God? What was the irony in the situation?

Expected answer: Lencho wrote to God asking for 100 pesos because hailstorm destroyed his crops. The irony is that he blamed the post office employees who actually collected and sent him the money.

Solution: Lencho had unshakeable faith in God. After the hailstorm destroyed his crops, he wrote to God for 100 pesos. The postmaster collected 70 pesos from employees and sent it. Lencho, receiving only 70, believed God sent 100 but post office staff stole 30. This is dramatic irony — he blames his helpers.

Example 2: What is the message of "Dust of Snow"? Explain with reference to literary devices.

Expected answer: Even small, seemingly insignificant natural events can change our mood and perspective. The poem uses symbolism, alliteration, and inversion.

Solution: The crow (usually a bad omen) shakes dust of snow from a hemlock tree (poisonous/negative) onto the poet — yet this simple event lifts his spirits. Frost uses traditionally negative symbols to create a positive outcome, showing that change can come from unexpected sources. Literary devices: symbolism (crow, hemlock), alliteration, inversion ("has given my heart / a change of mood").

Practice Questions

EASY Q1: Who was Lencho? What was his only hope?
Answer: Lencho was a poor farmer whose only hope was help from God after hailstorm destroyed his crops.
EASY Q2: What did the postmaster do after reading Lencho's letter?
Answer: He collected money from employees and sent 70 pesos to Lencho in an envelope signed "God".
EASY Q3: What does "fire" symbolise in "Fire and Ice"?
Answer: Fire symbolises desire, passion, and greed.
MED Q4: How is irony central to "A Letter to God"?
Answer: The irony is that Lencho blames the post office employees — the very people who helped him — for stealing money. His unshakeable faith becomes the source of injustice towards his benefactors.
MED Q5: Why are crow and hemlock significant in "Dust of Snow"?
Answer: Both are traditionally negative symbols — crow (bad omen) and hemlock (poisonous tree). Yet together they cause a positive change in the poet's mood, suggesting good can come from unexpected sources.
MED Q6: Compare fire and ice as agents of destruction in Frost's poem.
Answer: Fire (desire/passion) can consume and destroy through greed. Ice (hatred/indifference) can freeze and destroy through coldness. Both are equally destructive — Frost says ice (hatred) would "suffice" for destruction.
HARD Q7: Discuss the character of the postmaster. Was his act of kindness futile?
Answer: The postmaster showed great compassion and went beyond his duty. Though Lencho blamed him, the act was not futile — it demonstrated human goodness and preserved Lencho's faith in God, which was his source of strength.
HARD Q8: How does Robert Frost use nature as a metaphor for human emotions in both poems?
Answer: In "Dust of Snow," nature (crow, snow, hemlock) becomes a catalyst for emotional change. In "Fire and Ice," natural elements symbolise human emotions — fire for desire, ice for hatred. Frost shows that nature mirrors and influences our inner world.
HARD Q9: "Lencho's faith was both his strength and his weakness." Justify.
Answer: Strength: his faith gave him hope and courage to write to God in desperate times. Weakness: the same blind faith made him suspicious of innocent helpers and unable to appreciate human kindness. His faith blinded him to the goodness around him.
