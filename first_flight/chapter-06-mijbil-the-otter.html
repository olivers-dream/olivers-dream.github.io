Chapter Summary

Prose: Gavin Maxwell describes his experience of keeping an otter named Mijbil (Mij) as a pet. He obtained Mij from the marshes of Iraq and brought him to London. The story describes Mij's playful nature, his love for water, his inventive games with a ball, and the journey from Iraq to London (including a disastrous episode on the plane). Poem: "Fog" by Carl Sandburg is a short imagist poem comparing fog to a cat — it comes silently on "little cat feet," sits looking over the city on silent haunches, and then moves on.

  • Mijbil the otter: playful nature and love for water
  • Transporting Mij from Iraq to London — airline chaos
  • "Fog": imagist poem, fog as a cat on little feet
  • Themes: human-animal bond, nature observation
💡 Remember It Like This: Mij the otter was like a mischievous toddler in an adult's world — endlessly curious, loves water, makes a mess everywhere, destroys bathrooms, and completely wins your heart. Fog, like a cat, comes and goes as it pleases — you never notice until it's there.

🗺 Mind Map: Key Concepts

Central concept connects to main topics.

Core Concepts

  • Gavin Maxwell wanted a pet otter instead of a dog. Mij was a smooth-coated otter from Iraqi marshes.
  • Mij loved water — he turned on taps, played in bathtubs, and invented games with marbles and balls.
  • The air journey from Iraq to London was chaotic — Mij escaped in the plane and caused panic.
  • Mij was a new sub-species, later named Lutrogale perspicillata maxwelli after Maxwell.
  • In London, people couldn't identify Mij — guesses included baby seal, walrus, hippo, beaver.
  • "Fog" — only 6 lines; imagist poem with one extended metaphor (fog = cat).
  • No rhyme scheme, free verse; personification of fog sitting on haunches.
  • Carl Sandburg belongs to the imagist movement — clarity, precision, and economy of language.

Key Literary Terms

  • Free verse = poetry without regular meter or rhyme
  • Imagism = poetic movement emphasising precision, clarity, economy of language
  • Personification = giving human qualities to non-human things (fog sitting on haunches)
  • Extended metaphor = fog compared to a cat throughout the poem

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Confusing Gavin Maxwell (author/naturalist) with the otter's species name
  • Forgetting the air travel complications — this is an important episode in the chapter
  • Treating "Fog" as a nature poem only — it's also about how quietly change can arrive
  • Not recognising that Mij was a completely new sub-species of otter

Worked Examples

Example 1: Describe the challenges Gavin Maxwell faced while transporting Mij to London.

Expected answer: He couldn't take Mij on a passenger flight directly. During the journey, Mij escaped from the box, created chaos on the plane, and frightened passengers.

Solution: Maxwell had to find an airline that allowed animals. He kept Mij in a box with air holes. During the flight, Mij tore open the box and escaped, causing panic among passengers. An air hostess helped Maxwell catch Mij. The otter was covered in the lining of the box and was in a horrible mess. This shows the difficulty of keeping exotic pets.

Example 2: How does Carl Sandburg use metaphor in "Fog"?

Expected answer: He compares fog to a cat — it comes on "little cat feet," sits silently on haunches looking over the harbour, and then moves on.

Solution: The entire poem is one extended metaphor. Fog, like a cat, arrives quietly and unexpectedly ("on little cat feet"). It sits and observes ("looking over harbour and city on silent haunches"). It leaves without notice ("and then moves on"). Sandburg captures the transient, mysterious quality of fog through the familiar image of a cat.

Practice Questions

EASY Q1: Where did Maxwell get his otter from?
Answer: From the marshes of southern Iraq (Tigris marshes). His friend got it for him.
EASY Q2: What does the fog do in Sandburg's poem?
Answer: It comes on little cat feet, sits on silent haunches looking over the harbour and city, then moves on.
EASY Q3: What was special about Mij's species?
Answer: Mij was a new sub-species of otter, later named Lutrogale perspicillata maxwelli after Gavin Maxwell.
MED Q4: Describe Mij's playful nature with examples.
Answer: Mij loved water and would turn on taps to play. He invented games — juggling a small rubber ball, playing with marbles in the bathtub, and sliding on floors. He was endlessly curious and inventive.
MED Q5: Why do people fail to recognise Mij in London?
Answer: Otters are uncommon pets in London. People guessed he was a baby seal, beaver, walrus, bear cub, or even a hippo. This shows how unusual it was to keep an otter as a pet.
MED Q6: What qualities of a cat does Sandburg attribute to fog?
Answer: Silent movement (cat feet), quiet observation (sitting on haunches, looking), and sudden departure. Both come and go without announcement.
HARD Q7: What does the chapter reveal about the human-animal bond?
Answer: Maxwell's deep attachment to Mij — tolerating destroyed bathrooms, chaotic flights, and public confusion — shows the profound bond between humans and animals. Mij wasn't just a pet; he was a companion who brought joy and purpose. The chapter shows that love for animals transcends convenience.
HARD Q8: Compare "Fog" with "Dust of Snow" as nature poems.
Answer: Both are short and use nature to convey deeper meaning. "Dust of Snow" — a natural event changes the poet's mood (transformation). "Fog" — nature observed as metaphor (fog = cat). Both use economy of language but differ in impact: Frost's poem is about personal change, Sandburg's about quiet observation of nature.
HARD Q9: Analyse how Maxwell uses humour in narrating Mij's story.
Answer: Humour comes from Mij's antics — destroying the bathroom, causing airline panic, being mistaken for exotic animals. Maxwell describes chaotic situations calmly, creating comic effect. The contrast between human order (flights, London life) and Mij's wild unpredictability is inherently funny.
