Chapter Summary

"The Proposal" is a one-act farce by Anton Chekhov. Ivan Lomov, a nervous, hypochondriac landowner, visits his neighbour Stepan Chubukov to propose marriage to his daughter Natalya. However, before he can propose, Lomov and Natalya get into heated arguments — first about who owns Oxen Meadows, then about whose dog is better (Lomov's Guess vs Natalya's Squeezer). Lomov faints, is revived, and Chubukov quickly forces the marriage while they temporarily stop arguing. They begin fighting again immediately after the engagement. The play satirises the foolishness of the landed gentry and the triviality of their quarrels.

  • Lomov's proposal: practical, not romantic
  • Oxen Meadows and dogs: trivial arguments
  • Farce, satire, and exaggerated characters
  • Chekhov's critique of the landed gentry
💡 Remember It Like This: The Proposal is like two people who match perfectly on a dating app but can't stop arguing about trivial things at their first meeting — the wedding happens despite the fighting, and the fighting continues despite the wedding. Chekhov shows that some things never change.

🗺 Mind Map: Key Concepts

Central concept connects to main topics.

Core Concepts

  • Genre: one-act farce — a humorous play with exaggerated characters and absurd situations.
  • Ivan Lomov: 35-year-old hypochondriac, nervous, wants to marry Natalya for practical reasons.
  • Natalya Stepanovna: argumentative, proud, stubborn, but eager to marry when she discovers the proposal.
  • Stepan Chubukov: Natalya's father, initially welcoming but joins arguments; forces the marriage.
  • Three disputes: (1) Oxen Meadows ownership, (2) quality of dogs (Guess vs Squeezer), (3) continues even after engagement.
  • Chekhov satirises the Russian landed gentry's pettiness and obsession with property.
  • Dramatic irony: the audience knows Lomov came to propose, but he keeps getting sidetracked.
  • Themes: absurdity of human behaviour, pride, pettiness, and the institution of marriage.

Key Literary Terms

  • Farce = comedy with exaggerated situations and absurd plot
  • Hypochondriac = person who constantly worries about being ill
  • Satire = using humour, irony, exaggeration to criticise society
  • Dramatic irony = audience knows more than the characters

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Writing that Lomov actually proposes clearly — he never manages to propose properly; Chubukov forces it
  • Forgetting that it's a one-act play (no scene changes or intervals)
  • Missing that Lomov's marriage motive is practical (age, property, health), not romantic
  • Confusing the two dogs' names — Guess is Lomov's, Squeezer is Natalya's

Worked Examples

Example 1: What is the central conflict in "The Proposal" and how is it resolved?

Expected answer: Lomov comes to propose but gets into arguments about Oxen Meadows and dogs. He faints, and Chubukov forces the marriage while Lomov is barely conscious. They start arguing again immediately.

Solution: Lomov visits Chubukov's house to propose to Natalya. Before he can propose, they argue about who owns Oxen Meadows. Lomov leaves in anger. Natalya learns about the proposal and calls him back. They argue about their dogs (Guess vs Squeezer). Lomov faints from stress. Chubukov puts Natalya's hand in Lomov's and declares them engaged. They revive and immediately start arguing again. The "resolution" is comically incomplete.

Example 2: How does Chekhov use humour and satire in the play?

Expected answer: Through exaggerated characters, absurd arguments, physical comedy (fainting), and the irony that a proposal turns into endless bickering. He satirises the pettiness of the landed gentry.

Solution: Humour comes from: (1) Lomov's hypochondria — he constantly mentions his palpitations and numb leg. (2) Trivial arguments treated as life-and-death matters (Oxen Meadows, dogs). (3) Physical farce — Lomov faints, needs water, and is force-married while dazed. Satire targets the landed gentry's obsession with property and status over human relationships.

Practice Questions

EASY Q1: Why does Lomov visit Chubukov's house?
Answer: To propose marriage to Chubukov's daughter, Natalya Stepanovna.
EASY Q2: What are the two main arguments in the play?
Answer: First about the ownership of Oxen Meadows, then about whose dog is better (Lomov's Guess vs Natalya's Squeezer).
EASY Q3: What is a farce?
Answer: A type of comedy with exaggerated characters, absurd situations, slapstick humour, and improbable plot.
MED Q4: Why does Lomov want to marry Natalya? Is it for love?
Answer: No, not purely for love. Lomov is 35, suffers from palpitations and insomnia, and thinks he should settle down. His reasons are practical — he needs a companion and their properties adjoin each other.
MED Q5: Describe the character of Natalya Stepanovna.
Answer: Natalya is argumentative, proud, and stubborn — she fights fiercely about Oxen Meadows and dogs. But she's also eager for marriage — when she learns Lomov came to propose, she immediately wants him back. She's a complex mix of pride and practicality.
MED Q6: How does Chubukov react when Lomov first mentions his purpose?
Answer: Chubukov is overjoyed — he hugs Lomov, sheds tears, and gives his blessing. But his joy quickly turns to anger when the argument about Oxen Meadows begins.
HARD Q7: "The Proposal" shows that pride can destroy relationships. Discuss.
Answer: Both Lomov and Natalya are too proud to concede any point — ownership, dogs, anything. Their pride prevents the proposal from happening naturally. Even after engagement, they continue fighting. Chekhov shows that pride and stubbornness over trivial matters can overshadow genuine human connections.
HARD Q8: Analyse Chekhov's portrayal of marriage in "The Proposal."
Answer: Marriage is shown as a social and economic transaction, not a romantic ideal. Lomov wants marriage for practical reasons; Natalya wants it for social status. The actual moment of engagement is farcical (Lomov is unconscious). Chekhov critiques the institution by showing the couple can't even agree on small things — suggesting their married life will be perpetual conflict.
HARD Q9: Compare "The Proposal" with the letters in "A Letter to God" in terms of irony.
Answer: Both use situational irony. In "A Letter to God," Lencho's faith leads him to blame his helpers. In "The Proposal," a man who comes to propose ends up arguing instead, and the marriage happens by accident. Both stories show the gap between intention and outcome, and both use irony to expose human absurdity.
