Chapter Summary

Prose: The story of Gautama Buddha's journey from Prince Siddhartha to the Enlightened One. After witnessing suffering (old age, sickness, death), he renounced his princely life to seek truth. After enlightenment, he preached his first sermon at Benares. The chapter focuses on the story of Kisa Gotami, who brings her dead son to Buddha hoping for a cure. Buddha asks her to bring mustard seeds from a house where no one has died — she cannot find one, and thus learns that death is universal and grief must be overcome. Poem: "For Anne Gregory" by W.B. Yeats is about love and appearance — a young woman says she'll change her hair colour so someone will love her for herself, not for her looks. The poet argues only God can love us for our inner selves alone.

  • Siddhartha to Buddha: four sights and the Middle Path
  • Kisa Gotami and the universality of death
  • "For Anne Gregory": love, appearance, and the inner self
  • Acceptance as a path to peace
💡 Remember It Like This: Kisa Gotami's search for mustard seeds is like going door to door asking families who haven't experienced loss — you quickly realise every household carries its own grief. This is not depressing; it's liberating because shared suffering connects us all.

Core Concepts

  • Prince Siddhartha saw four sights: old man, sick man, funeral, monk — leading to his quest.
  • He attained enlightenment under the Bodhi tree at Bodhgaya, became the Buddha (the Awakened One).
  • First sermon at Benares (Sarnath) is called "Dhammacakkappavattana Sutta."
  • Kisa Gotami's story: teaches that death is universal, no house is free from loss.
  • The mustard seed parable: grief is eased by accepting the universality of death.
  • "For Anne Gregory" — love should not be based on external beauty alone.
  • The honey-coloured hair symbolises external beauty that attracts superficial love.
  • Yeats uses a dialogic structure — the old man (poet) argues with the young woman.

Key Literary Terms

  • Parable = a short story teaching a moral or spiritual lesson
  • Four Noble Truths: suffering exists, suffering has a cause, suffering can end, the path to end suffering
  • Dialogic poem = poem structured as a conversation between two speakers
  • Rhyme scheme of "For Anne Gregory": ABCABC

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Writing Kisa Gotami got the mustard seeds — she could NOT find a house untouched by death
  • Confusing the Four Noble Truths with the Eightfold Path
  • Missing that Yeats's poem is about the impossibility of being loved purely for inner self (only God can)
  • Treating the sermon as just a religious text — it teaches about universal grief and acceptance

Worked Examples

Example 1: What lesson did Kisa Gotami learn from her search for mustard seeds?

Expected answer: She learned that death is universal — no household is free from loss. She must accept death as inevitable and overcome grief.

Solution: Buddha asked Kisa Gotami to bring mustard seeds from a house where no one had died. She went from door to door but every family had lost someone. She realised death is not personal punishment but a universal truth. She returned to Buddha, understood his lesson, and accepted her son's death. The lesson: clinging to grief is futile; acceptance brings peace.

Example 2: What is the central argument of "For Anne Gregory"?

Expected answer: The poet argues that people are loved for external appearance, not inner self. Only God can love someone purely for who they are inside.

Solution: Anne Gregory says she can dye her hair to be loved for herself, not her appearance. The poet (old man) argues that young men love her yellow hair (external beauty), and changing it won't change human nature. He concludes with a religious text saying only God can look past appearance to love the soul. The poem questions whether purely selfless love is humanly possible.

Practice Questions

EASY Q1: What were the four sights that changed Prince Siddhartha's life?
Answer: An old man, a sick man, a funeral procession, and a monk. These made him aware of suffering and inspired his spiritual quest.
EASY Q2: What did Buddha ask Kisa Gotami to bring?
Answer: Mustard seeds from a house where no one had ever died.
EASY Q3: What colour is Anne Gregory's hair in the poem?
Answer: Honey-coloured (yellow/golden) hair.
MED Q4: Why could Kisa Gotami not find a single mustard seed as Buddha asked?
Answer: Because every household she visited had experienced death. She could not find a home untouched by loss. This made her understand that death is universal.
MED Q5: How did Siddhartha become the Buddha?
Answer: After leaving his palace and wandering for years, he meditated under a peepal tree at Bodhgaya and attained enlightenment. He then became "the Buddha" (the Awakened/Enlightened One).
MED Q6: Explain the message of "For Anne Gregory."
Answer: The poem says people judge and love based on appearance (hair colour, looks). True love based only on inner self is rare, perhaps only possible for God. Even if Anne changes her hair, people will love the new appearance, not necessarily her soul.
HARD Q7: What are the main teachings of Buddha's first sermon at Benares?
Answer: The world is full of suffering. Suffering is caused by desire and attachment. Suffering can be overcome by following the right path. One should avoid extremes (neither luxury nor extreme austerity) and follow the Middle Path. Death and impermanence are inevitable; accepting this brings peace.
HARD Q8: "Kisa Gotami's journey is every human's journey." Discuss.
Answer: Like Kisa Gotami, we all experience loss and initially refuse to accept it. We search for exceptions, hoping our grief is unique and reversible. But eventually, we realise death and suffering are universal. Accepting this truth doesn't remove pain but transforms it into compassion and understanding.
HARD Q9: Compare the theme of acceptance in "The Sermon at Benares" with "For Anne Gregory."
Answer: Both deal with accepting uncomfortable truths. Buddha teaches acceptance of death and impermanence. Yeats suggests accepting that human love is influenced by appearance. Both argue that resisting these truths causes suffering. In both, wisdom comes from acknowledging reality rather than clinging to illusions.
