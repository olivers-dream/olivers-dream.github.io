Chapter Summary

Trace how modern nation-states emerged in Europe after the French Revolution. Understand the ideas of liberalism, conservatism, and romanticism, and how nationalist movements reshaped the map of Europe in the 19th century.

  • French Revolution and Napoleonic ideas of nationalism
  • Liberalism vs Conservatism in 19th century Europe
  • Unification of Italy (Mazzini, Cavour, Garibaldi) and Germany (Bismarck)
  • Romanticism and the Balkans crisis

Core Concepts

  • French Revolution (1789) introduced the idea of the nation as a sovereign community of citizens with equal rights.
  • Napoleon spread nationalism through the Civil Code (1804): abolished privileges, equality before law, uniform weights/measures.
  • Liberalism: freedom of individual, equality before law, consent-based government, free markets.
  • Conservatism (post-1815): monarchies restored at Congress of Vienna. Metternich dominated. Censorship imposed.
  • Revolutionaries: Giuseppe Mazzini (Young Italy), secret societies, 1848 revolutions across Europe.
  • Unification of Italy: Mazzini (ideals) → Cavour (diplomacy) → Garibaldi (military). Completed 1861.
  • Unification of Germany: Otto von Bismarck, "blood and iron" policy, three wars, unified 1871 under Prussian king.
  • Romantic nationalism: folk culture, language (Grimm brothers), music used to build national identity.
  • Balkans became flashpoint: Ottoman decline, competing Slavic nationalisms, led towards World War I.

Key Dates & Facts

  • French Revolution: 1789
  • Napoleon Civil Code: 1804
  • Congress of Vienna: 1815
  • Italy unified: 1861
  • Germany unified: 1871

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Confusing Mazzini (ideals), Cavour (diplomacy), Garibaldi (military)
  • Mixing up 1830 and 1848 revolutions
  • Thinking nationalism was always peaceful — Balkans show its dark side

Worked Examples

Example 1: How did Napoleon spread nationalism?

Expected answer: Through Civil Code: equality before law, abolished privileges, uniform systems across conquered territories

Solution: Napoleon carried French Revolution ideas to other countries. Civil Code gave equality, ended feudalism, standardised weights and measures. Initially welcomed, later resented as foreign rule.

Example 2: Who unified Germany and how?

Expected answer: Bismarck through "blood and iron" policy — three wars against Denmark, Austria, France

Solution: Bismarck was Prussian chief minister. Believed in realpolitik. Won against Denmark (1864), Austria (1866), France (1870). Unified Germany under Prussian king Wilhelm I in 1871.

Practice Questions

EASY Q1: When was the French Revolution?
Answer: 1789
EASY Q2: Who founded Young Italy?
Answer: Giuseppe Mazzini
EASY Q3: What was Napoleon's Civil Code?
Answer: Law code establishing equality before law, property rights, abolished birth-based privileges.
MED Q4: What was the Congress of Vienna?
Answer: Meeting of conservative powers in 1815 to restore monarchies and undo Napoleonic changes. Hosted by Metternich.
MED Q5: What is liberalism in the context of 19th century Europe?
Answer: Freedom of individual, equality before law, government by consent, end of autocracy, free trade across regions.
MED Q6: Compare the roles of Mazzini, Cavour, and Garibaldi in Italian unification.
Answer: Mazzini: revolutionary ideals, inspired youth. Cavour: diplomatic alliances (with France). Garibaldi: military campaigns in southern Italy.
HARD Q7: Why did the 1848 revolutions fail?
Answer: Lack of coordination, conservative forces regrouped, middle class feared working class demands, no unified leadership.
HARD Q8: How did romanticism help nationalism?
Answer: Folk songs, local language, cultural traditions made people feel connected. Grimm brothers' fairy tales, Karol Kurpinski's music in Poland created shared identity.
HARD Q9: Why were the Balkans a source of intense nationalism?
Answer: Ottoman decline created power vacuum. Slavic nations claimed independence. Russia, Austria, Germany competed for influence. Led to WWI.
