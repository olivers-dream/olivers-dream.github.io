Core Concepts

  • Silk Routes: ancient trade networks connecting Asia, Europe, Africa — goods, ideas, diseases travelled.
  • Pre-modern world: trade in textiles, spices; spread of religions (Buddhism, Islam) along trade routes.
  • Colonialism: Europeans conquered Americas, Africa, Asia for gold, raw materials, slave trade.
  • Columbian Exchange: food (potatoes, tomatoes, maize) travelled from Americas to Europe; diseases (smallpox) devastated native populations.
  • 19th century: Industrial Revolution → mass production → need for raw materials and markets → global trade boom.
  • Indentured labour: Indian workers went to Caribbean, Fiji, Mauritius under harsh contracts — "new slavery".
  • Great Depression (1929): US stock market crash → global trade collapsed → unemployment, poverty worldwide.
  • Bretton Woods (1944): IMF and World Bank created. Fixed exchange rates. Dollar as anchor currency.
  • Post-war: decolonisation, new nations joined global economy. MNCs emerged in 1950s-60s.

Key Dates & Facts

  • Great Depression: 1929-1930s
  • Bretton Woods Conference: 1944
  • IMF & World Bank established: 1944
  • Corn Laws repealed: 1846

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Thinking globalisation started only recently — Silk Routes were ancient globalisation
  • Forgetting the role of disease in colonisation
  • Mixing up IMF (stability) and World Bank (development)

Worked Examples

Example 1: What was the Bretton Woods system?

Expected answer: Post-WWII economic order: IMF for monetary stability, World Bank for reconstruction, fixed exchange rates tied to dollar

Solution: Created in 1944 at Bretton Woods, USA. Aimed to prevent another Great Depression. Dollar linked to gold. Other currencies pegged to dollar. Lasted until 1971.

Example 2: How did the Great Depression affect India?

Expected answer: Agricultural prices crashed, farmers couldn't sell crops, rural India devastated

Solution: India was exporter of raw materials. When world trade collapsed, prices fell 50%. Peasants couldn't repay debts. Urban India less affected. Jute exports declined.

Practice Questions

EASY Q1: What were the Silk Routes?
Answer: Ancient trade networks connecting Asia, Europe, and Africa for goods, ideas, and culture.
EASY Q2: Name two Bretton Woods institutions.
Answer: IMF (International Monetary Fund) and World Bank
EASY Q3: What was the Columbian Exchange?
Answer: Transfer of plants, animals, and diseases between Americas and the rest of the world after Columbus.
MED Q4: What was the Corn Laws movement?
Answer: British landowners protected domestic grain. Industrialists wanted free trade for cheap food. Corn Laws repealed 1846 → food imports from USA, Australia.
MED Q5: Explain the concept of indentured labour.
Answer: Workers recruited under contract for plantations in Caribbean, Fiji, Mauritius. Harsh conditions, low pay, restricted freedom — called "new slavery".
MED Q6: How did the Great Depression start?
Answer: US stock market crash (1929) → banks collapsed → factories closed → unemployment → reduced global trade → worldwide depression.
HARD Q7: How did 19th-century technology transform global trade?
Answer: Railways connected hinterlands to ports. Steamships reduced travel time. Telegraph enabled communication. Refrigerated ships carried perishable food across oceans.
HARD Q8: Why did the Bretton Woods system collapse?
Answer: US printed too many dollars (Vietnam War spending). Couldn't maintain gold-dollar link. Nixon ended gold convertibility in 1971.
HARD Q9: Compare pre-modern and modern globalisation.
Answer: Pre-modern: slow, limited to luxury goods, cultural exchange. Modern: rapid, industrialised, mass production, exploitation of colonies, institutional framework (IMF, WTO).
