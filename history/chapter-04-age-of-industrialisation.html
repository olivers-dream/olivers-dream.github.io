Core Concepts

  • Proto-industrialisation: production for international market without factories (17th-18th century). Merchants gave raw material to rural workers.
  • Factory system: cotton and metal industries in Britain. Richard Arkwright's cotton mill (1781) — production under one roof.
  • Steam engine (James Watt), Spinning Jenny (James Hargreaves) — key inventions. But adoption was gradual, not sudden revolution.
  • Workers resisted machines: feared unemployment. Luddites destroyed machines. Women and children exploited.
  • Indian textiles dominated world trade before colonisation: muslin (Dhaka), calico (Calicut), chintz.
  • British colonial policy: destroyed Indian handicrafts through tariffs. India became raw material supplier.
  • Indian industrialisation (late 19th century): Dwarkanath Tagore, Jamsetji Tata (TISCO), Seth Hukumchand.
  • Swadeshi movement boosted Indian industry. Nationalist vision: self-reliance, Indian goods.
  • Small-scale and handloom sector survived alongside factories — dual economy.

Key Dates & Facts

  • Arkwright cotton mill: 1781
  • Spinning Jenny: 1764
  • TISCO (Tata Iron & Steel): 1907
  • First cotton mill in Bombay: 1854

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Thinking Industrial Revolution was sudden — it was gradual
  • Forgetting that handloom survived alongside factories
  • Assuming only Britain industrialised — India had proto-industrialisation too

Worked Examples

Example 1: What was proto-industrialisation?

Expected answer: Production for global market in homes/workshops, not factories; merchants coordinated rural producers

Solution: Before factories, merchants took orders from international traders, gave raw materials to peasant families. Products from countryside, not cities. Common in 17th-18th century Europe.

Example 2: How did colonialism deindustrialise India?

Expected answer: Tariffs on Indian goods, flooding India with British manufactured products, turning India into raw material supplier

Solution: British imposed heavy import duties on Indian textiles in Britain. Flooded Indian markets with machine-made British cloth. Indian weavers lost livelihood. India exported raw cotton, imported finished cloth.

Practice Questions

EASY Q1: What was the Spinning Jenny?
Answer: Machine invented by James Hargreaves (1764) that could spin multiple spindles at once.
EASY Q2: What is proto-industrialisation?
Answer: Production for international market through rural households, before the factory system.
EASY Q3: Who established TISCO?
Answer: Jamsetji Tata (founded in 1907 at Jamshedpur)
MED Q4: Why did workers oppose machines?
Answer: Feared job losses. Machines replaced skilled workers. Women and children hired cheaply. Luddites attacked factories.
MED Q5: Name two famous Indian textiles from pre-colonial era.
Answer: Muslin (Dhaka) and Calico (Calicut)
MED Q6: How did Swadeshi movement help Indian industry?
Answer: Boycott of foreign goods → increased demand for Indian products → boosted Indian mills and factories.
HARD Q7: Was the Industrial Revolution really a "revolution"?
Answer: Not sudden — new technologies adopted slowly, traditional industries persisted, change happened over decades. More an evolution than revolution.
HARD Q8: How did E.T. Paull's music book cover symbolise industrialisation?
Answer: Angel of progress with railways, factories, telegraph representing "dawn of the century" — technology as civilisation's march forward.
HARD Q9: Compare Indian and British industrialisation.
Answer: Britain: machines, factories, mass production, global markets. India: colonial constraints, limited industrialisation, textile handicrafts declined, Swadeshi provided impetus.
