Chapter Summary

Study the Indian national movement from the First World War to the Civil Disobedience Movement. Understand Gandhian methods: Non-Cooperation, Salt March, and how diverse groups united against colonial rule.

  • WWI aftermath, Rowlatt Act, Jallianwala Bagh
  • Non-Cooperation and Khilafat movements
  • Civil Disobedience and the Salt March
  • Dalit perspective (Ambedkar) and Muslim League concerns

Core Concepts

  • WWI, Rowlatt Act (1919), and Jallianwala Bagh massacre aroused mass nationalism.
  • Non-Cooperation Movement (1920): Gandhiji called for boycott of schools, courts, councils, foreign cloth.
  • Khilafat Movement: alliance between Congress and Muslim leaders to oppose British treatment of Ottoman Caliphate.
  • Chauri Chaura incident (1922): violence led Gandhiji to call off Non-Cooperation.
  • Simon Commission (1928): all-British, no Indian members → widespread protests.
  • Civil Disobedience Movement (1930): Salt March (Dandi March) — Gandhiji walked 240 miles to make salt.
  • Salt became symbol: breaking unjust law through non-violent civil disobedience.
  • Participation: peasants (no revenue), tribal (forest laws), workers, women, students.
  • Dalit perspective: Ambedkar demanded separate electorates; Poona Pact (1932) reserved seats within Hindu electorate.
  • Muslim League: feared Hindu dominance, demanded separate nation → partition backdrop.

Key Dates & Facts

  • Rowlatt Act: 1919
  • Non-Cooperation: 1920-22
  • Simon Commission: 1928
  • Dandi March: 12 March 1930
  • Gandhi-Irwin Pact: 1931
  • Poona Pact: 1932

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Confusing Non-Cooperation (1920) and Civil Disobedience (1930)
  • Forgetting Chauri Chaura reason for calling off movement
  • Thinking all groups had same goals — Dalits and Muslims had separate concerns

Worked Examples

Example 1: Why did Gandhiji call off Non-Cooperation?

Expected answer: Chauri Chaura violence: mob burnt police station killing 22 policemen

Solution: Feb 1922: crowd in Chauri Chaura set fire to police station. Gandhiji believed non-violence was non-negotiable. Movement withdrawn despite being at its peak.

Example 2: What was the significance of the Salt March?

Expected answer: Symbolised defiance of unjust British law through peaceful civil disobedience

Solution: Started 12 March 1930, Sabarmati to Dandi (240 miles). Making salt broke the Salt Law. Simple act that everyone could relate to. Thousands followed, made international headlines.

Practice Questions

EASY Q1: When was the Jallianwala Bagh massacre?
Answer: 1919 (13 April, Amritsar)
EASY Q2: What was the Dandi March?
Answer: Gandhiji's 240-mile walk from Sabarmati to Dandi to make salt, breaking the Salt Law.
EASY Q3: What was the Rowlatt Act?
Answer: British law allowing detention without trial and trial without jury — called "Black Act"
MED Q4: Why was Khilafat Movement important?
Answer: United Hindu-Muslim masses under Congress. First time Gandhiji brought Muslims into national movement.
MED Q5: What was the Poona Pact?
Answer: Agreement (1932) between Gandhiji and Ambedkar: reserved seats for Dalits within general electorate instead of separate electorates.
MED Q6: How did tribal communities participate in Civil Disobedience?
Answer: Tribals violated forest laws, entered reserved forests. In Gudem Hills, Alluri Sitarama Raju led armed tribal revolt.
HARD Q7: Why were some Muslim leaders reluctant to join Civil Disobedience?
Answer: Fear of Hindu majority rule, Congress-Muslim League differences, separate electorate demand, unresolved communal issues.
HARD Q8: Evaluate the role of women in the national movement.
Answer: Participated in protests, picketed liquor shops, went to jail. But mostly supportive roles. Few in leadership. Gandhiji saw women as symbols of sacrifice.
HARD Q9: How did the national movement unite and divide people simultaneously?
Answer: United: against colonial rule (salt, boycott). Divided: caste (Ambedkar), religion (Muslim League), class (workers vs industrialists had different demands).
